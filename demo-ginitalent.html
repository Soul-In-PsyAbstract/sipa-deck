Product vision
Tessera is the social platform where your standing in a community is yours to keep — reputation, governance rights, and creator earnings move with you, so communities can self-govern and creators can build a real business instead of chasing an algorithm.

This is not a real client engagement — it's a self-directed case study built to show exactly how I'd approach the Product Lead role described in the posting: owning vision and roadmap, running discovery-to-delivery, prioritizing with a defensible framework, and tying every initiative back to a metric. "Tessera" is a hypothetical product; the thinking underneath it is how I actually work.

Scope Web3 social platform · community spaces · creator monetization · on-chain reputation Horizon Next 2 quarters (Q3–Q4) Format Vision → prioritization → roadmap → metrics

Strategic pillars — next 2 quarters

Four bets, in priority order

Web3 social platforms fail for the same reason most social platforms fail — weak trust, no durable creator economics, and governance that's decorative rather than functional. These four pillars are chosen to close those gaps in order, not all at once.

01

Trust & identity infrastructure

Sybil-resistant onboarding and portable, on-chain reputation. Without this, every other pillar inherits a fake-account problem — so it's funded and shipped first, even though it's the least visible pillar to users.

02

Community governance tooling

Token-gated spaces, proposal/voting, and treasury visibility for community leads — the primitives that let a community actually run itself instead of depending on platform staff.

03

Creator monetization rails

Stablecoin tipping, subscriptions, and revenue splits with a real fiat off-ramp. Sequenced after trust and governance, because payouts on top of a sybil-prone graph just subsidize fraud.

04

Trust & safety at scale

Moderation tooling and abuse controls that compound as the graph grows — built alongside, not bolted on after growth makes it an emergency.

Prioritization framework

RICE applied to five real feature candidates

RICE = (Reach × Impact × Confidence) ÷ Effort. Reach is estimated users touched per quarter (thousands); Impact is 3/2/1/0.5 (massive/high/medium/low); Confidence is a percentage; Effort is person-months. The ranking below is deliberately counter-intuitive: the flashiest Web3 feature (governance voting) ranks last, and unglamorous trust infrastructure ranks first — that's the point of running the framework instead of building on instinct.

Rank Feature candidate Reach Impact Confidence Effort RICE score
1 Sybil-resistant onboarding (proof-of-personhood) 25,000 2 65% 3 10,833
2 Token-gated community spaces 15,000 2 75% 3 7,500
3 Portable on-chain reputation badges 25,000 2 70% 6 5,833
4 Stablecoin creator payouts (tips + subs) 8,000 3 80% 4 4,800
5 Governance proposal & voting module 5,000 1 60% 5 600
Reading the table: onboarding wins on reach and effort even at middling confidence and impact — it's cheap, touches everyone, and unlocks everything downstream. Payouts score high per-user (Impact 3) but touch a smaller base early. Governance voting is high-effort, lower-confidence (early communities don't reliably use formal voting), and low-reach until spaces mature — it's real, but it's a Q4/Later bet, not a launch feature.

Roadmap

Now / Next / Later

Sequenced directly off the RICE ranking above — not a wishlist reshuffled to look tidy. Each phase unlocks the reach or confidence numbers the next phase depends on.

NOWWeeks 1–6
Sybil-resistant onboarding

Proof-of-personhood integration + progressive trust scoring. Ships before anything that pays out value.

Token-gated spaces v1

NFT/token allowlist gating for community entry. Minimum viable governance surface.

NEXTWeeks 7–14
Portable reputation badges

Cross-community reputation primitive built on the trust-scoring foundation shipped in Now.

Stablecoin creator payouts

Tipping + subscriptions with a vetted fiat off-ramp partner. Gated behind onboarding to limit payout fraud.

LATERQ4+
Governance proposal & voting

Treasury-linked, community-led decision making — introduced once communities have enough reputation-weighted members to make votes meaningful.

Reputation interoperability

Open import/export standard so reputation earned on Tessera has value beyond it — the long-term moat.

How I'd measure success

Metrics tied to the strategy, not vanity numbers

A Web3 social platform can post impressive MAU while the actual economy underneath is hollow. These are the numbers I'd put in front of leadership every two weeks.

Engagement

  • WAU/MAU ratio, tracked by cohort — not just headline MAU
  • Community formation rate: number of spaces with 20+ weekly active members
  • Session frequency segmented by onboarding-verified vs. unverified users

Retention

  • D7 / D30 retention, split by whether a user completed reputation onboarding
  • Creator retention specifically — churn here is a leading indicator, not a lagging one
  • Cohort retention curves by acquisition channel

Creator economics

  • Total creator payout volume and month-over-month growth
  • % of active creators earning above a meaningful monthly threshold
  • Take-rate sustainability: platform revenue vs. payout-to-GMV ratio

Trust & safety

  • Sybil detection rate at onboarding vs. post-hoc removal rate
  • Moderation actions per 1,000 active users, trended over time
  • Community health score derived from report-rate trend, not raw volume
North star

Weekly Active Contributing Members (WACM) — users who post, vote, tip, or govern in a given week, not just view. It's a deliberately stricter bar than MAU, chosen because a platform built on reputation and governance only works if "active" means "contributing," not "scrolling."

A note on this exercise

Why I built this instead of a generic pitch

Tessera doesn't exist — I built this case study to demonstrate the actual mechanics of the role: framing a defensible vision, running a real prioritization framework instead of gut-feel roadmapping, sequencing a roadmap that respects dependencies between initiatives, and defining metrics before the first line of code ships.

For a real platform, this same process starts with discovery — user interviews, usage data, technical constraints from engineering, and the actual token/treasury economics already in place — none of which I could fabricate honestly for a hypothetical product. What's fixed here is the method; what changes with the real platform is the inputs.
